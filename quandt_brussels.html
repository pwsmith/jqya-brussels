Suppletion

What is Suppletion?

  • Most basically suppletion refers to the following
    • A lexical item has two or more forms.
    • The forms are phonologically unrelated.
    • Use determined by morphological context.
  • Examples:

      • person~people

    • *persons, change determined by plurality

      • gut~besser~(am) besten

    • *guter, am gutsten, changed determined by comparative, superlative

Suppletion

  • Suppletion is the very definition of irregular patterns.
  • As such, it makes a good testing ground for UG.
  • The extent to which patterns can be learned tells us...

Adjectival Suppletion

  • Bobaljik (2012) looks at patterns of suppletion in adjectives, for comparative and superlative degrees.

Missing Patterns

  • But, there are two patterns that are not attested:

Positive Comparative Superlative Pattern
good better goodest ABA
good gooder best AAB

  • Generalisations:
    • If the comparative is suppletive, the superlative is also suppletive.
    • For the superlative to be suppletive, then comparative must also be.

Robustness

  • How robust is this?
    • Very.
    • Data drawn from over 300 languages, representing 40+ families.
    • Total dataset = 100 distinct cognate triples
    • The use of cognate triples ensures that English good~better~best and Dutch goed~beter~best are treated as the same data point.

Summary

  • ABA and AAB are logically possible patterns.
  • There is no a priori reason why they shouldn't exist.
  • The patterns are clearly learnable, so why do they not arise?

Explanation: Bobaljik

  • Adjective, Comparative and Superlative are in a containment relation.
  • The superlative cannot appear without the comparative.
  • Sup → Comp

Pronominal case

Morphological Case

  • Roughly speaking, case encodes the roles that items play in sentences.
  • In German, usually:
    • Nominative = subject
    • Accusative = (direct) object
    • Dative = goal/indirect object
  • Case has been argued to be complex, in a manner akin to what Bobaljik proposes for adjective gradation (Caha 2009).

Bulding the survey

  • We looked for patterns suppletion for case in pronouns (we~us).
  • In order to be instructive, languages need to show 3+ case distinctions.
  • We ordered the cases on a hierarchy following Marantz (1991):
    • Nominative--Accusative--Dative
    • Absolutive--Ergative--Dative
    • Since this doesn't matter, I'll use Case 1, Case 2, Case 3

General Discussion

Consequences

  • What does any of this mean?
  • An alternative is to learn all irregularity.
  • That is, the 'default' state of the learner is to regularise rules.
  • If a rule does not match the regular pattern, then the irregular patterns are stored.

Benefit

  • The benefit of doing this is that we don't treat suppletion as a special case.
  • All irregularity is then reduced to 'learning what deviates from the norm'
  • For instance, leaf~leaves is irregular, but hardly fitting the 'extreme' definition of suppletion.
